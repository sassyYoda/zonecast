# Listener Profile

> **This is a template.** Copy it to `listener.md` (`zonecast profile init` does this for you)
> and rewrite it to describe yourself. The real `listener.md` is gitignored — it's your
> private state, not code. Every value below is a placeholder; the *structure* is what
> matters, because SKILL.md reads this file before every planning and drafting pass.

There is exactly one listener. Write for them, not for an audience. Second person ("you")
throughout — this is a private tutorial, not a broadcast.

## Who they are

- A graduate-level researcher or practitioner in the field they want episodes about.
  Replace this with your actual role, and say what kind of material is most valuable to
  you right now (e.g. fundamentals reviews, canonical papers, a specific subfield).
- Learns best by listening to people explain things; that's the whole reason this system exists.
- Baseline that can be safely assumed without explanation:
  - List the math and theory you already own. Anything listed here is never re-explained.
  - List the domain fundamentals you already own — the concepts an episode may open with
    and build on top of, rather than building up to.
  - List adjacent tooling/engineering context you're comfortable with.
- Baseline that should NOT be assumed: name the boundaries — niche subfield details, any
  specific paper's contribution, recent techniques, anything outside your home field.
  Build these from the ground up when they appear.
- "From the ground up" therefore means: from this baseline up. Never explain the things in
  the assumed list; always explain what a new abstraction is *doing* before using it.
- Calibrate up, not down. When in doubt whether something needs explaining, spend one
  sentence on it rather than three minutes or zero seconds.

## The listening conditions

Describe when and how you actually listen — this is what makes the audio constraints in
SKILL.md load-bearing rather than decorative.

What this means concretely:

- **Session length.** State your typical range. Duration offers in Pass 1 should span it,
  and episodes should match session length when you mention the activity.
- **Cognitive headroom.** Say what else is competing for attention. If attention dips
  rhythmically or gets interrupted, the recap cadence and rewind-proofing rules are
  load-bearing here, not nice-to-haves.
- **Hands and eyes.** If you can't pause, rewind, or look anything up, say so plainly —
  the whole design assumes the play button is pressed once.
- **Late-episode fatigue.** If working memory degrades late in a long session, say so:
  long episodes will front-load the hardest material and shift toward story, implications,
  and synthesis in the back half.
- Occasional light nods to your setting are welcome and humanizing, but keep them rare —
  once or twice an episode, never a running bit.

## Tone calibration for this listener

- Peer-to-peer, not lecturer-to-student. Treat them like a smart colleague who happens not
  to know this particular thing yet.
- Enthusiasm is welcome, hype is not. "This result is genuinely weird and I want you to
  feel how weird" — yes. "This CHANGES EVERYTHING" — no.
- Intellectual honesty over smoothness: flag open problems, contested claims, and "nobody
  actually knows why this works" moments explicitly. This listener will trust the episode
  more, not less.

## Maintaining this file

When you reveal durable new facts (a subfield you've now mastered, a new phase with
different session lengths, feedback like "too slow" / "too fast"), update this file rather
than relying on conversation memory.
