# Anti-patterns: how not to sound generated

Injected at drafting and polish time as a companion to `style-guide.md` §9. These are the
machine defaults to actively resist *as you write* — not rules to check afterward. Each is a
reflex a language model reaches for; each has a fix. A concrete example follows the list,
because seeing the bad-to-good shift moves your defaults more than any rule.

## The reflexes to catch in yourself

- **Naming the feeling.** "something beautiful falls out," "here's the genuinely strange part,"
  "sit with that," "the fascinating thing is." → Cut it. Build the beat and let the listener's
  own reaction arrive. Say what to notice, never how to feel about it.
- **Announcing before asserting.** "here's where it gets interesting," "let's dive in," "it's
  important to note that." → Delete the frame; say the thing. If it's interesting they'll notice.
- **The stock opener.** Do **not** open with "Here's a thing that shouldn't be possible," "Here's
  the thing," "Imagine a world where," "What if I told you," or any interchangeable hook that
  would fit any topic. Open on the *specific* concrete image, number, or scene of THIS subject.
- **"Here's the—" as connective glue.** You will reach for "Here's the X" at every seam. Use it
  once, maybe. Then vary or just start the next sentence.
- **The reflexive antithesis.** "It's not X, it's Y," "not the count, not the size — just Z." At
  most **once** per episode, and only to dislodge a belief the listener actually holds. Elsewhere,
  assert Y and delete the "not X."
- **The metronome.** Long explanatory sentence, then a punchy fragment, then long, then fragment —
  predictable alternation is not rhythm. And do not lean on one-word fragments ("Good." "Except.")
  as your section-seam beat; ration them to one per section.
- **No real long sentence.** Machines avoid the long build. Each section needs at least one
  genuinely long, clause-stacked sentence (~30–40 words) that earns its length walking through a
  process or piling up a case — then break to something short. Without it, everything is
  medium-length and the prose drones.
- **Frictionless perfection.** You never stumble. Real explanation does. At least once, genuinely
  self-interrupt and repair in the text ("the cache — no, better: think of it as a notebook the
  processor keeps on its desk") — the TTS won't improvise the stumble, so write it.
- **The analogy that never leaks.** If your spine analogy maps perfectly start to finish, it's too
  clean to be true. Find where it stops fitting and say so out loud — that's usually the best beat.
- **Ending on a life lesson.** Don't close by reframing the mechanism as a truth about the self
  ("your own tolerance for being wrong, turned into switches"). End on the mechanism or a real
  open question. The last line is content, not a mic-drop.
- **Book-voice diction.** delve, realm, tapestry, testament, underscore, paramount, myriad, foster,
  leverage, utilize, robust, seamless, harness. Nobody says these aloud. Use the plain verb.

## Do NOT over-correct into sterility

Scrubbing too hard yields a lifeless narrator, which is just a different machine tell. Keep:
contractions, opinions ("this is the part everyone gets wrong"), the odd "basically/kind of,"
sentences that open with And/But/So, one strong analogy per concept, and genuinely uneven
enthusiasm. The target is one specific person thinking out loud — loose, particular, occasionally
stumbling — not clean.

## One bad-to-good rewrite (study the shift)

**Generated (four tells stacked — named feeling, stock opener, mirrored template, fragment tic):**
> So where is it? Where do those two forces cancel? Here's the click, and it's cleaner than you'd
> expect. Fewer stampers than that, and the wall's too empty. More stampers than that, and the
> wall's too crowded. Dead center is the sweet spot. That's the peak.

**Human (invites the listener to verify; adds friction and one real long build; rations fragments):**
> So where does that balance actually sit? I'll give you the answer, then I want you to check
> whether it feels right, because it surprised me the first time. The two forces cancel out right
> when the wall is half-lit. That's it. Why there? Because too few stampers wastes all that dark
> space you could have used to catch a false yes, while too many floods the wall so fast that a lit
> switch tells you almost nothing — half of them are lit anyway, so a "yes" is basically a coin
> landing heads three times, which happens. Somewhere between wasteful and crowded, the wall's
> exactly half full.

What changed: the self-congratulation ("cleaner than you'd expect") became an invitation to
verify; the mirrored "Fewer… / More…" template became one long clause-stacked sentence carrying
both cases with a concrete image (the coin) at the end; the three stacked fragments collapsed to a
single deliberate "That's it."
